# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Students self-studying for the Cambridge A2 Key exam. Primarily teenagers and young adults, often on mobile, practising solo at home. The app is a standalone tool — no teacher facilitation, no account, no onboarding needed. The user arrives already motivated; the job is to practise, submit, and see results without friction.

## Product Purpose

A realistic, mobile-first mock test for the Cambridge A2 Key Reading & Writing paper. Students complete all 7 parts under timed conditions, then review scored results. Success looks like: a student finishes a full test run, sees where they went wrong, and feels ready to sit the real exam.

## Brand Personality

Clear · Calm · Trustworthy. The interface should reduce test anxiety rather than add to it. It should feel reliable and official — closer to the actual Cambridge paper than to an edtech startup — without being cold or bureaucratic.

## Anti-references

- **No gamification** (Duolingo, Kahoot): no streaks, coins, XP bars, mascots, confetti bursts, or reward loops. This is exam practice, not a game.
- **No corporate LMS** (Moodle, Canvas, Blackboard): no grey sidebars, no nested menus, no course-catalogue chrome.
- **No heavy dashboards**: no widgets, no data density, no admin-tool aesthetic. The user has one job per session — take the test.

## Design Principles

1. **Test conditions first** — every visual decision should ask "does this distract from answering the question?" If yes, remove or quiet it.
2. **Mobile native, not mobile adapted** — layout and interactions are designed for a phone held in one hand, not shrunken from desktop.
3. **Calm authority** — the palette and typography convey the seriousness of exam preparation without intimidating the student.
4. **Progressive disclosure** — show only what the current part needs. No peeking ahead, no backwards navigation during an active test.
5. **Honest feedback** — results are clear, unambiguous, and educational. No softening wrong answers with vague encouragement.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Touch targets ≥ 44px. Sufficient colour contrast for correct/incorrect states (not colour-only). Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. English-language learners are the primary audience — UX copy should be simple, direct, and jargon-free.
